Epigenetics and behavior

Epigenetic mechanisms have attracted great interest in the study of behavior and mental disorders.

They help explain how experiences, especially early in life,
can have long-lasting effects on brain function.

Epigenetic mechanisms can explain:

  • Why early experiences can have lifelong effects
  • Why stress can also shape behavior

Epigenetic mechanisms allow:

  • Transient experiences
  • To produce long-term biological effects

They provide a way for plasticity
to be maintained over time.

Epigenetic mechanisms

There are several epigenetic mechanisms. Two of the most studied are:

  • Histone acetylation
  • DNA methylation

Epigenetics was originally developed
to explain how cellular traits are inherited
without changes in DNA sequence.

This was crucial in:

  • Developmental biology
  • Cell differentiation

During development we have undifferentiated cells (stem cells):

  • Cells are initially totipotent
  • Then become multipotent
  • Finally become specialized

This occurs through epigenetic regulation of gene expression. As cells encounter specific signals:

  • Some genes are silenced
  • Others are activated

The gene remains present, but epigenetic modulate its expression.

Epigenetics in neurons

In neurons:

  • Epigenetic changes are maintained
    at the level of the individual cell
  • They provide long-term regulation of neuronal function.

Epigenetic mechanisms are now considered crucial for:

  • Long-term behavioral change
  • Resilience and vulnerability
  • Adaptive and maladaptive plasticity

They link experience to lasting brain change.

Epigenetic mechanisms are processes that:

  • Alter gene expression
  • Without changing the DNA sequence

They act through chromatin remodeling.

Chromatin

Think of chromatin as a spool of thread:

  • Thread = DNA
  • Spool = histone proteins

DNA wraps around histones.

Chromatin is the combination of:

  • DNA
  • Histone proteins

Its structure determines whether genes
are accessible for transcription.

Chromatin states

Chromatin can be:

State Transcription
Inactive Not accessible
Repressed Low
Permissive Medium
Active High

DNA methylation

DNA methylation:

  • Adds a methyl group (CH3) to DNA
  • Acts as a transcriptional stop signal

When methylation is present:

  • Transcription machinery is blocked

Life experiences can modify:

  • Which regions of DNA are methylated

This changes transcription probability
without altering DNA sequence.

Studies of monozygotic twins show:

  • At early age, epigenetic patterns are similar
  • Later in life, patterns diverge

Experience leaves an epigenetic signature.

Evidence from twins

Histone modifications

Histone proteins are not static.

They can undergo post-translational modifications after they are synthesized.

These modifications change how histones interact with DNA.

A histone modification is a chemical change added to histone proteins.

These changes:

  • Do not alter DNA sequence
  • Alter chromatin structure
  • Modify gene transcription probability

They act as regulatory signals.

Histone modifications determine:

  • How tightly DNA is packed
  • Whether a gene is accessible

They regulate gene expression dynamically.

Histone modifications

Common histone post-translational modifications include:

  • Acetylation
  • Methylation
  • Phosphorylation
  • Ubiquitination

Histone modifications do not act in isolation.

Each modification can alter chromatin structure or recruit specific regulatory proteins. In a much more complex way. Different combinations of modifications
create a histone code. This code:

  • Fine-tunes gene expression
  • Allows flexible and context-dependent regulation

Histone modifications

Histone acetylation is one type of histone modification. It consists of adding an acetyl group to specific regions of histone proteins.

Histone acetylation:

  • Makes chromatin less compact
  • Increases DNA accessibility
  • Increases Genes expression

Chronic social defeat stress

Chronic social defeat stress is an animal model
used to study stress, depression-like behavior,
and resilience.

The model involves:

  • A dominant male mouse
  • A subordinate male mouse

The subordinate mouse is placed
in the cage of the dominant one.

Persistent stress exposure

  • The dominant mouse forces subordination
  • The subordinate experiences social defeat

This interaction lasts:

  • 10 minutes per day
  • For 10 consecutive days

When separated:

  • The mice remain in visual contact
  • They can smell each other

Stress therefore continues
even outside direct interaction.

Only protocols lasting at least 10 days:

  • Induce stable depression-like behavior
  • Produce anhedonia-like symptoms

Shorter protocols are insufficient.

Chronic social defeat produces:

  • Strong experience of subordination
  • Reduced motivation for social interaction

This is measured quantitatively.

Measuring anhedonia

Anhedonia is assessed using a
social interaction task.

The task measures:

  • Time spent exploring a conspecific
  • Willingness to engage socially

Compared to controls:

  • Defeated mice explore less
  • Show social withdrawal

The distribution shifts toward
reduced social interaction.

Individual differences

After social defeat:

  • Susceptible mice show social withdrawal
  • Resilient mice behave like controls

There is a continuum between the two poles.

Effect of chronic stress

What determines vulnerability or resilience?

Genetic differences are unlikely:

  • These mice belong to the same strain

This points to non-genetic mechanisms.

Differences may arise from:

  • Epigenetic regulation
  • Experience-dependent plasticity

Under normal conditions:

  • The BDNF gene is highly expressed
  • Chromatin is open
  • Histones are acetylated

This supports hippocampal plasticity.

After chronic social defeat:

  • Chromatin becomes more compact
  • Histone acetylation decreases

BDNF expression and plasticity in the hippocampus are reduced.

Effect of chronic stress

Reduced hippocampal BDNF leads to:

  • Reduced neurogenesis
  • Reduced synaptic plasticity
  • Impaired hippocampal function

This contributes to anhedonic behavior.

Antidepressant treatment

After antidepressant treatment:

  • Histone acetylation increases
  • BDNF transcription partially recovers

However:

  • DNA methylation remains

Stress-induced epigenetic changes:

  • Persist after stress ends
  • Are only partially reversible

These changes act as molecular scars.

Molecular Scar

If antidepressant treatment stops:

  • BDNF reduction reappears

The epigenetic trace of stress remains.

Chronic stress:

  • Alters neural circuits
  • Leaves latent molecular marks

Recovery is possible,
but not complete.

VTA–NAc circuit and resilience

The hippocampal effects are not the only mechanism involved.

After social defeat vulnerable mice show:

  • Hyperactive VTA dopamine neurons
  • NAc becomes overstimulated

Resilient mice:

  • Upregulate potassium channels in the VTA
  • Reduce dopamine neuron firing preventing chronic NAc overstimulation.

The activation of NAc follows a homeostatic principle:

  • Acute dopamine → pleasure
  • Chronic dopamine → saturation → anhedonia

Active resilience

These findings indicate that resilience is not merely the absence of adverse effects. Rather, it reflects an active process, with specific mechanisms shaping distinct outcomes.

Can resilience be induced?

Yes.

Vulnerable animals can become resilient
by targeting adaptive mechanisms.

Ezogabine, a drug enhancing potassium channels:

  • Reduced depressive and anhedonic symptoms
  • Normalized reward circuit connectivity

Plasticity needs guidance

Drugs can increase plasticity. But plasticity is undirected. The environment determines the outcome.

SSRIs increase serotonin rapidly. Clinical effects appear slowly. This suggests involvement of slow plastic mechanisms.

SSRIs:

  • Increase neural plasticity
  • Create a window for change

The environment drives whether changes are
adaptive or maladaptive.

Fluoxetine-treated mice:

  • Improved in enriched environments
  • Worsened in stressful environments

The drug increased sensitivity to context.

Early developmental environment and epigenetics

Early life experiences can have long-lasting effects on behavior. These effects are often mediated by epigenetic mechanisms.

M. Meaney studied differences in maternal behavior in rats.

Mothers were classified based on:

  • Licking and grooming
  • Nursing posture

They are better described as high-nurturing or low-nurturing mothers.

Early developmental environment and epigenetics

Low-nurturing mothers:

  • Feed offspring normally
  • Are not abusive

The difference lies in interaction quality, not basic care.

Rats raised by low-nurturing mothers:

  • Are more neophobic
  • Have a chronically active HPA axis
  • Females often become low-nurturing mothers.

Rats raised by high-nurturing mothers:

  • Are less neophobic
  • Have a well-regulated HPA axis
  • Females often become high-nurturing mothers.

The stress system: HPA axis

The HPA axis involves:

  • Hypothalamus → CRH
  • Pituitary → ACTH
  • Adrenal glands → cortisol

Cortisol feeds back to the hippocampus.

The hippocampus contains
glucocorticoid receptors (GR).

More GRs means:

  • Faster negative feedback
  • Better stress regulation

Epigenetic programming of stress

Maternal care activates epigenetic mechanisms at the GR gene in the hippocampus.

High GR expression leads to:

  • Efficient HPA self-regulation
  • Lower anxiety
  • Lower cortisol levels

Low maternal care produces the opposite pattern. Early experience programs
the long-term functionality of the HPA axis.

Functional recovery

After TSA treatment:

  • HPA axis responses normalized
  • Stress reactivity decreased

The negative effects of low maternal care were significantly reduced.

Similar recovery occurs with:

  • Enriched environments
  • Foster care

Epigenetic scars may persist,
but their functional impact can be reduced.
