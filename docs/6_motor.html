What is the motor system

The motor system allows the nervous system to generate movement. It transforms goals and intentions into coordinated muscle activity.

Producing movement requires:

  • Selecting an action
  • Planning its structure
  • Executing motor commands
  • Monitoring outcomes

These processes are distributed across multiple brain regions.

Key components of the motor system include:

  • Motor cortex
  • Basal ganglia
  • Cerebellum
  • Brainstem
  • Spinal cord

They interact continuously during movement.

Primary motor cortex

The primary motor cortex (M1) is the main cortical output of the motor system. It is located in the frontal lobe, anterior to somatosensory cortex. Neurons in M1 are the upper motor neurons and send commands toward the spinal cord.

Primary motor cortex is organized somatotopically. Different body parts are represented in different cortical regions. Fine motor control occupies more cortical space.

Encode patterns of movement such as:

  • Direction
  • Force
  • Coordination

Movement emerges from population activity.

Signals from motor cortex descend to the spinal cord. These pathways convey voluntary motor commands. Control is largely contralateral.

The spinal cord is the main site of motor execution. It integrates descending commands with sensory input.

From cortex to muscle

Lower motor neurons reside in the ventral horn of the spinal cord. They are the final common pathway for movement. Their axons exit the CNS to innervate muscles.

Motor commands travel from:

  • Motor cortex (Upper MN)
  • Through descending pathways
  • To spinal motor neurons (Lower MS)
  • To muscle fibers

This pathway transforms intention into force.

M1 is not enough

Although M1 and the spinal cord can generate movement, they do not decide what movement to make. That requires additional motor areas.

Voluntary movement involves choices.

The nervous system must:

  • Select appropriate actions
  • Organize sequences
  • Suppress unwanted movements

Planning occurs before execution.

The supplementary motor area (SMA) supports internally generated actions.

It is critical for:

  • Self-initiated movement
  • Sequencing
  • Bimanual coordination

It organizes actions over time.

The premotor cortex contributes to planning movements guided by external cues. It links sensory information to motor commands. It prepares movements before they are executed.

M1 is not enough

Subcortical structures

The basal nuclei regulate whether an action is initiated.

They are involved in:

  • Selecting appropriate actions
  • Suppressing competing or unwanted actions

They do not generate movement or send commands to muscles. Their role is to gate motor plans.

The cerebellum regulates how an action is executed.

It is involved in:

  • Coordination and timing
  • Error correction
  • Motor learning

It compares intended movement with actual outcome.

Cortical damage

Damage to primary motor cortex produces:

  • Weakness or paralysis
  • Loss of fine voluntary control
  • Deficits on the contralateral side

Reflexes are generally preserved.

Damage to premotor cortex or supplementary motor area leads to:

  • Difficulty planning movements
  • Impaired sequencing or initiation
  • Apraxia despite preserved strength

The problem is planning, not execution.

Basal nuclei lesions

Damage to the basal nuclei disrupts action selection.

This can result in:

  • Difficulty initiating movement
  • Excessive or involuntary movements

Strength is usually preserved.

Basal nuclei dysfunction in Parkinsonian disorders leads to:

  • Bradykinesia
  • Rigidity
  • Resting tremor

Movement is difficult to initiate, not to execute.

Cerebellar damage

Damage to the cerebellum causes:

  • Poor coordination
  • Inaccurate movements
  • Timing deficits

Strength is preserved, but movement is imprecise.

Cerebellar ataxia:

  • Unsteady gait
  • Dysmetria
  • Intention tremor

Errors increase during movement.

Motor neuron lesions

Damage to upper motor neurons leads to:

  • Weakness
  • Increased muscle tone
  • Hyperreflexia

This reflects loss of inhibitory control.

Damage to lower motor neurons causes:

  • Flaccid paralysis
  • Muscle atrophy
  • Loss of reflexes

The final output pathway is disrupted.

Spinal cord and peripheral nerve damage

Spinal cord lesions produce:

  • Weakness below the lesion
  • Patterned sensory and motor deficits
  • Preserved reflexes below the lesion

The level of the lesion matters.

Damage to peripheral nerves results in:

  • Weakness in specific muscles
  • Loss of reflexes

Deficits follow peripheral anatomy.
