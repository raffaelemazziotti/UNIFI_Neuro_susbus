What is Resilience

The term resilience originally comes from metallurgy.

It refers to the ability of a material
to resist forces applied to it without breaking.

In this sense, resilience is the opposite of fragility.

In psychology, resilience does not mean simply resisting damage.

Resilience is the ability to:

  • React actively to adverse or traumatic events
  • Reorganize one’s life in a positive way
  • Continue developing despite difficulties

Resilience involves adaptation, not endurance alone.

The Kauai longitudinal study

The concept of resilience entered psychology through a landmark longitudinal study.

Werner (1993) followed the entire cohort of children born in 1955 on the island of Kauai (about 700 individuals, for more than 40 years).

About 1/3 of these children were exposed to:

  • Social disadvantage
  • Family instability
  • Environmental stress

As a group, this population showed a higher incidence of psychological distress.

Despite high risk exposure:

  • One third of these children developed normally
  • They formed stable relationships
  • Showed good work performance
  • Demonstrated sociability and altruism

These individuals were defined as resilient.

What differentiated resilient individuals
from those who succumbed to early adversity?

From risk to protection

Possible factors included:

  • Genetics
  • Life experiences
  • Protective environmental factors

These findings shifted research focus:

From identifying risk factors
to identifying protective factors.

The question became:

How does the brain adapt successfully to adversity?

To study mechanisms of resilience,
animal models are essential.

One widely used model is social defeat stress.

Resilience as an active process

Resilience is not simply the absence of pathology. It involves active and adaptive processes. These include specific molecular and circuit-level changes.

Resilience involves both:

  • Passive resilience
    Absence of maladaptive molecular responses
    seen in vulnerable individuals

  • Active resilience
    Presence of adaptive plastic changes
    seen only in resilient individuals

Both forms of resilience depend on neuroplasticity.

Plastic changes allow the brain to:

  • Adjust stress responses
  • Reorganize circuits
  • Maintain functional behavior

Resilience is biologically implemented.

Genetic factors and behavior

Understanding how genetic factors contribute
to behavior and its disorders is a central problem in neuroscience.

Caspi et al. (2006) described three main approaches to study this relationship:

  • Gene-to-disorder approach
  • Endophenotype-to-disorder approach
  • Gene–environment interaction approach

Gene-to-disorder

The gene-to-disorder approach assumes:

  • A direct, linear relationship between genes and disorders
  • Genes are the primary cause of disorders

This approach used:

  • Linkage analysis
  • Association studies

It searched for correlations between:

  • Psychiatric diagnoses
  • Individual differences in DNA sequence

Although a few genes showed replicated associations:

  • Replication failures were common
  • Progress was slow and inconsistent

Many researchers abandoned the search
for direct main effects of genotype on diagnosis.

Endophenotype-to-disorder

The endophenotype-to-disorder approach introduced
intermediate phenotypes.

Endophenotypes are heritable biological traits associated with disorders.

They can be:

  • Physiological
  • Biochemical
  • Anatomical

Example: macrocephaly or immune alterations in ASD.

This approach assumes that:

  • Endophenotypes have clearer genetic bases
  • Genes exert stronger effects on intermediate traits
    than on complex diagnoses

Gene–environment interaction (G × E)

The gene–environment interaction (G × E) approach
explicitly includes environmental factors.

The G × E approach assumes that:

  • Environmental risk factors can contribute to disorders
  • Genotype modulates susceptibility to these factors

Genes influence sensitivity to the environment.

According to the G × E framework:

  • There may be no gene-behavior association
    in the absence of environmental stressors

  • There may be no environmental effect
    without genetic susceptibility

Risk emerges from their interaction.

Genes as risk factors

In this view:

  • Genes are not causative
  • They increase the probability of developing a disorder

Only under specific environmental conditions.

Genetic and environmental factors:

  • Are both risk factors
  • Act together
  • Increase vulnerability when combined

Exposure does not guarantee pathology.

Genes:

  • Define neural circuit potential
  • Also define limits of environmental influence

The environment:

  • Acts on genetic circuits
  • Shapes them toward maturity

Donald Hebb summarized this relationship clearly. Asked whether genes or environment matter more, he replied: Is the height or the width more important to calculate the area of a rectangle?

Polymorphisms as risk and protective factors

A polymorphism is a variation in DNA sequence present in more than 1% of the population.

Polymorphisms can involve:

  • Substitution of DNA bases
  • Deletion of DNA bases
  • Insertion of DNA bases

They do not cause disease by themselves.

Polymorphisms can act as:

  • Risk factors
  • Protective factors

Their effect depends on the environment.

This fits the gene–environment interaction framework.

Childhood maltreatment and antisocial behavior

Childhood maltreatment is a strong and universal risk factor for antisocial behavior. However:

  • Not all maltreated children develop antisocial outcomes
  • Responses to maltreatment vary widely

This variability suggests biological moderation.

Caspi et al. (2002) focused on the MAOA gene.

The MAOA gene:

  • Is located on the X chromosome
  • Codes for the enzyme monoamine oxidase A

The MAOA enzyme is involved in the catabolism of monoamines:

  • Dopamine
  • Norepinephrine
  • Serotonin

It makes these neurotransmitters inactive.

MAOA polymorphism

A functional polymorphism affects MAOA activity. Low-activity MAOA variants:

  • Degrade monoamines less efficiently
  • Allow neurotransmitters to persist longer
  • Increase synaptic signaling

In mice:

  • Silencing the MAOA gene increases aggression
  • Restoring MAOA expression normalizes aggression

This shows a causal link in regulating aggression.

In humans:

  • A rare null mutation of MAOA was linked
    to antisocial behavior in a Dutch family

Because MAOA is X-linked:

  • Affected males had no functional enzyme
  • This acted as a human knockout

This mutation is extremely rare.

In most individuals:

  • MAOA is present
  • But its activity varies

The Dunedin longitudinal study

Caspi et al. tested this hypothesis
in the Dunedin Multidisciplinary Health and Development Study.

This cohort includes:

  • 1,037 individuals
  • Born in 1972–1973 in Dunedin, New Zealand

Participants were followed from childhood into adulthood.

The study had three major strengths:

  • The cohort represents the general population.
  • Well-characterized adversity
  • Antisocial behavior was assessed using multiple sources

Gene-environment interaction

In the absence of maltreatment:

  • Both MAOA genotypes showed low antisocial behavior

The polymorphism alone was not sufficient.

When maltreatment occurred:

  • Antisocial behavior increased in all individuals
  • The increase was much stronger
    in low-activity MAOA individuals

The curves diverged only under adversity.

If the environment is protective:

  • The genotype does not produce antisocial behavior

Maltreatment is necessary for the genetic effect to emerge.

Although only 12% of males had both:

  • Low-activity MAOA
  • Childhood maltreatment

They accounted for 44% of violent convictions.

Gene-environment interaction

Among severely maltreated males:

  • 85% with low-activity MAOA
    developed antisocial behavior

Risk increased, but outcomes were not inevitable.

Pathogenesis is multifactorial.

It is:

  • Not purely genetic
  • Not purely environmental

Risk emerges from their interaction.

Life stress and depression

Across the lifespan, stressful life events involving:

  • Threat
  • Loss
  • Humiliation
  • Defeat

strongly influence the onset and course of depression.

However, not everyone exposed to stress becomes depressed.

Caspi et al. proposed that:

  • Stressful life events act as an environmental risk factor
  • Genetic variation moderates individual sensitivity to stress

This follows the gene–environment interaction framework.

The candidate gene: 5-HTT

The gene under study codes for the serotonin transporter (5-HTT). The transporter regulates serotonin reuptake at the synapse.

The serotonin system is relevant to depression because:

  • It is the target of SSRI antidepressant drugs
  • It modulates mood, stress reactivity, and emotion regulation

This makes 5-HTT a plausible candidate gene.

The 5-HTT gene contains a polymorphism in its promoter region
called 5-HTTLPR. Two main variants exist: Long (l) and Short (s) allele.

The short allele is associated with:

  • Lower transcriptional efficiency
  • Reduced serotonin transporter expression

Genotypes:

  • l/l: high transporter expression
  • s/l: intermediate expression
  • s/s: low expression

Non-human primates

In rhesus macaques:

  • The 5-HTTLPR polymorphism is analogous to humans

The short allele is associated with:

  • Reduced serotonergic function
  • Abnormal HPA response

Only in monkeys reared in stressful conditions No effect is seen in normally reared monkeys.

The Dunedin study on MDD

Different types of stressful life events between ages 21 and 26 were assessed. Among participants:

  • 30% experienced no stressful events
  • 20% experienced two events
  • 11% experienced three events
  • 15% experienced four or more events

There was:

  • No significant main effect of genotype
  • A significant gene–environment interaction

As the number of stressful life events increased:

  • Risk of depression increased in all individuals
  • The increase was much steeper in s/s individuals
  • l/l individuals were relatively protected

In the absence of stressful life events: All genotypes showed similar levels of depression
The gene alone was not sufficient.

No stress, no difference

Suicide and early life adversity outcomes

Similar interaction effects were observed for:

  • Suicidal ideation
  • Suicide attempts

Stress predicted these outcomes mainly in s/s individuals.

The interaction also showed a correlation with early life adversities:

  • Childhood maltreatment predicted adult depression
  • Only in individuals carrying an s allele
  • Not in l/l homozygotes

Dopamine and reward sensitivity

Individual differences in reward sensitivity
can be partly influenced by genetic variation.

One candidate gene is the DRD4 gene.

The DRD4 gene codes for the D4 dopamine receptor.

This receptor is involved in:

  • Dopamine signaling
  • Pleasure and gratification
  • Motivation and reward sensitivity

The DRD4 gene shows a functional polymorphism. Two main variants are often considered:

  • Long (L) allele
  • Short (S) allele

The L variant is considered hypofunctional.

The DRD4 polymorphism appears to influence the salience of appetitive stimuli

Alchol Experiment

Experimental Design
Alcohol study

Participants
Young adults

Conditions

  • One session with 3 alcoholic drinks
  • One session with 3 non-alcoholic control drinks
  • The order of conditions was counterbalanced

Results
Participants with the L/L genotype: reported a stronger desire to continue drinking, but only after the alcoholic condition Participants with the S/S genotype showed no difference between conditions.

Not only for Alcohol

Other GxE interactions: Cannabis Use

Early use of cannabis, especially during adolescence, is associated with an increased risk of psychosis.

This includes:

  • Transient psychotic symptoms
  • Clinically relevant disorders
  • Diagnosis of schizophrenia

But most individuals who use cannabis do not develop psychosis. This suggests that cannabis exposure alone
is not sufficient.

One candidate gene is COMT.

The COMT gene codes for catechol-O-methyltransferase.

This enzyme is involved in the degradation of Dopamine

COMT activity is particularly important
in the prefrontal cortex.

A functional polymorphism exists in the COMT gene:

  • MET allele (high activity)
  • VAL allele (low activity)

Other GxE interactions: Cannabis Use

Experimental Design
Working Memory and Cannabis study

Participants
Young adults

Procedure

  • A small dose of cannabis
  • Placebo
  • Perform a working memory task.

Results
In VAL/VAL individuals:

  • Baseline performance was good
  • Cannabis markedly impaired performance

An evolutionary question

If some polymorphisms increase vulnerability, why have they not been eliminated by evolution?

Early gene-environment interactions

Gene-environment interactions are especially powerful
during early development.

Early experiences can shape long-term cognitive
and emotional outcomes.

One of the most influential projects in this area is the
Bucharest Early Intervention (BEI) Project.

This study examined the effects of early psychosocial deprivation
on child development.

The BEI Project studied children raised in
Romanian orphanages.

These children experienced:

  • Severe social deprivation
  • Limited caregiver interaction

Bucharest Early Intervention Project

Randomized early-life intervention

Experimental design

Children were randomly assigned to:

  • Remain in institutional care
  • Be placed in foster care
Comparison groups

Three groups were compared:

  • Institutionalized children
  • Foster care children
  • Never institutionalized children

This design allowed causal inference.

Cognitive outcomes
  • Cognitive performance was consistently higher in foster care
  • Outcomes were better when fostering occurred earlier

Early intervention mattered.

A genetic moderator

Despite overall benefits, children differed in their degree of recovery.

Drury et al. (2010) investigated whether genetic variation
could explain differences in recovery. They focused on the COMT val158met polymorphism.

The study included 136 children from the BEI Project. Procedure:

  • At 2.5 years: children assigned to foster care or institution
  • At 4.5 years: psychiatric assessment
  • Genotyping for COMT val158met

Among children who remained institutionalized:

  • Carriers of the MET allele showed fewer depressive symptoms
  • VAL/VAL homozygotes showed higher symptoms

Among children placed in foster care:

  • VAL/VAL children improved dramatically
  • Their depressive symptoms dropped
  • They reached levels similar to MET/MET children

Positive environments had a strong impact.

A key insight

The children who appeared most vulnerable
benefited the most from a supportive environment.

This is not simple vulnerability. This is plasticity.

This study was among the first to show:

  • Genetic differences predict recovery
  • Positive environments can override early adversity
  • G × E applies to improvement, not only to risk

Many G × E studies focused mainly on:

  • Stress
  • Negative life events

They paid less attention to positive environments.

The differential susceptibility model
(Belsky et al., 2007; Belsky & Pluess, 2009)
proposes a different perspective.

The differential susceptibility model

Individuals differ in their susceptibility to:

  • Negative environments
  • Positive environments

The same genotype can amplify both.

Genetic variants are better described as:

  • Plasticity variants

Rather than:

  • Risk genes
  • Vulnerability genes

They increase environmental responsiveness.

The idea that individuals are genetically determined
(except for rare genetic diseases) is incorrect.

Open questions

We still do not fully understand:

  • Why some genotypes are more plastic
  • Which neural circuits are involved
  • How genes and environments interact at the brain level

Progress requires studies linking:

  • Genetic variation
  • Environmental exposure
  • Brain mechanisms
  • Behavioral outcomes

Across development.

Conclusions

Mental disorders and individual differences
must be studied through gene–environment interactions.

We cannot change genes. But we can improve environments. Prevention is a powerful intervention.
