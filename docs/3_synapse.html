3. Synaptic Communication

Neurons transmit information to other neurons and target cells using specialized compartments called Synapses. A Synapse is a specialized junction where a neuron communicates with another neuron (or another cell).

IMPORTANT: In synapses, neurons are not physically continous, but there is a gap called Synaptic Cleft

Three main components:

  • Presynaptic compartment (or terminal)
  • Synaptic cleft
  • Postsynaptic compartment (or spine)

Synapses allow:

  • Directional signaling
  • Signal modulation
  • Integration of multiple inputs

3. Presynaptic Events

  1. Action potential arrives at terminal
  2. Membrane depolarization
  3. Opening of voltage-gated Ca²⁺ channels
  4. Ca²⁺ influx triggers vesicle fusion
  5. Neurotransmitter released into synaptic cleft

3. Synaptic Vesicles

  • Small membrane-bound organelles in the presynaptic terminal
  • Store neurotransmitters at high concentration
  • Cluster near active zones of the presynaptic membrane

Key properties:

  • Release occurs in discrete, quantal events
  • The release is mediated by SNARE proteins
  • Vesicles are recycled locally

Process:

  • SNAREs bring vesicle and presynaptic membranes close together
  • Ca²⁺ influx triggers final fusion step
  • Neurotransmitter released by exocytosis

3. Postsynaptic Membrane

Contains neurotransmitter Receptors

Receptors can be of two main classes:

  • Ionotropic receptors
    • Ligand-gated ion channels
    • Fast responses
  • Metabotropic receptors
    • G-protein coupled
    • Slower but modulatory (long-lasting response)

Postsynaptic Events

Neurotransmitter binding produces graded signals of different types:

  • EPSP: Excitatory PostSynaptic Potential
  • IPSP: Inhibitory PostSynaptic Potential

Excitatory synapses:

  • Depolarize the membrane
  • Mediated by Na (or Ca²⁺ in special cases) influx

Inhibitory synapses:

  • Hyperpolarize or stabilize membrane potential
  • Mediated by Cl⁻ influx

Neurotransmitter action ends by:

  • Reuptake into presynaptic terminal
  • Enzymatic degradation

Neurotransmitters

Neurotransmitters are chemical messengers released at synapses
They transmit signals from a presynaptic neuron to a postsynaptic cell

Each neurotransmitter:

  • Is synthesized in the neuron
  • Is released in response to activity
  • Acts on specific receptors

Neurotransmitters can be grouped by function:

  • Excitatory
  • Inhibitory
  • Modulatory

The effect depends on the receptor, not only on the neurotransmitter

Glutamate - Glu

Main excitatory neurotransmitter in the brain

  • Increases neuronal excitability (EPSP)
  • Really abbundant in the brain

Acts on:

  • Ionotropic receptors
  • Metabotropic receptors

GABA

Main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain

  • Reduces neuronal excitability (IPSP)
  • Essential for network stability

Acts on:

  • Ionotropic receptors (fast inhibition)
  • Metabotropic receptors (slow inhibition)

Acetylcholine - ACh

Acts as a Diffuse Projection System in the brain. This means that is produced in specific nuclei and projected throughout the brain. AKA: Cholinergic system

Involved in:

  • Muscle contraction
  • Vital and Cognitive functions

Acts on:

  • Ionotropic receptors
  • Metabotropic receptors

Serotonin - 5-HT

Acts as a Diffuse Projection System in the brain. This means that is produced in specific nuclei and projected throughout the brain. AKA: Serotoninergic system

Involved in:

  • Mood and Emotional processing
  • Vital functions including Sleep and Appetite

Acts on:

  • Ionotropic receptors
  • Metabotropic receptors

Dopamine - DA

Acts as a Diffuse Projection System in the brain. This means that is produced in specific nuclei and projected throughout the brain. AKA: Dopaminergic system

Involved in:

  • Motivation, Learning and Reward
  • Movement control

Acts on:

  • Metabotropic receptors

Noradrenaline - NA

Or Norepinephrine. Acts as a Diffuse Projection System in the brain. This means that is produced in specific nuclei and projected throughout the brain. AKA: Noradrenergic System.

Involved in:

  • Arousal and Attention
  • Stress response

Acts on:

  • Metabotropic receptors

Adrenaline

Or Epinephrine. Less abundant in the Central Nervous System than other neurotransmitters. More Peripheral.

Involved in:

  • Arousal and Autonomic regulation
  • Stress response

Acts on:

  • Metabotropic receptors

Other Signaling Molecules

In addition to classical neurotransmitters, neurons use other chemical signals.
These molecules often act as modulators rather than fast transmitters

Neuropeptides

  • Endorphins
    • Pain, Reward, Stress regulation …
  • Oxytocin
    • Social, Maternal behavior …
  • Substance P
    • Pain, Inflammatory responses …

Other Signaling Systems

  • Endocannabinoids
    • Appetite, mood, and memory, Retrograde signaling ….
  • Nitric Oxide (NO)
    • Retrograde and Short-range signaling, Blood flow regulation …

Receptors

Neurotransmitters act by binding to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane
Receptors determine the speed and effect of synaptic transmission

Ionotropic Receptors

  • Ligand-gated ion channels
  • Neurotransmitter binding directly opens the channel
  • Produce fast responses (milliseconds)

Metabotropic Receptors

  • Do not form ion channels
  • Coupled to intracellular signaling pathways
  • Neurotransmitter binding activates G-proteins
  • Effects are slower but longer-lasting

Glutamate Ionotropic Receptors

Glutamate activates multiple ionotropic receptors, including:

  • AMPA receptors
    • Fast excitatory transmission
    • Allow Na⁺ influx
    • Main mediator of rapid excitation
  • NMDA receptors
    • Slower and Long-lasting kinetics
    • Permeable to Ca²⁺
    • Involved in synaptic plasticity

GABA Ionotropic Receptors

GABA-A receptors

  • Ionotropic inhibitory receptors
  • Allow Cl⁻ influx
  • Reduce neuronal excitability

Provide fast inhibition in neural circuits

Metabotropic Receptors

  • Do not form ion channels
  • Coupled to intracellular singaling pathways
  • Neurotransmitter binding activates G-proteins
  • Effects are slower but longer-lasting

Signaling Pathway: chain of intracellular events where molecules relay messages from outside the cell to trigger specific internal responses like growth, division, or death

Metabotropic glutamate receptors (mGluRs)

  • Modulate synaptic transmission
  • Regulate ion channel activity indirectly
  • Influence plasticity and excitability

Similar mechanisms apply to many modulatory systems systems, such as dopamine and serotonin

Neuromodulation is the process by which a neurotransmitter does not directly trigger a fast electrical signal, but instead changes how a neuron or a network responds to external inputs.

Position of the Synapse

Also the position of a synapse along a neuron matters, because neurons are not electrically uniform cables. Where a signal arrives strongly shapes what it can do.

  • Axo-dendritic
    • Axon contacts a dendrite (Most common type)
    • Often excitatory
  • Axo-somatic
    • Axon contacts the cell body
    • Strong influence on spike generation
  • Axo-axonic
    • Axon contacts another axon
    • Modulates neurotransmitter release

Neurotransmitter Clearance

After release, neurotransmitters must be removed from the synaptic cleft
This ensures that synaptic signals are brief and precisely controlled

  • Reuptake
    • Neurotransmitter is transported back into the presynaptic terminal
    • Mediated by specific membrane transporters
    • Allows recycling of neurotransmitter molecules
  • Enzymatic Degradation
    • Neurotransmitter is broken down by enzymes in the synaptic cleft
    • Prevents prolonged receptor activation

Drugs and Neurotransmitter Clearance

Many drugs act by altering how neurotransmitters are removed from the synaptic cleft
By changing clearance, drugs modify the strength and duration of synaptic signaling.

Selective Serotonin Reuptake Inhibitors (SSRIs)

  • Block serotonin reuptake transporters
  • Increase serotonin availability at synapses

Used to treat:

  • Depression
  • Anxiety disorders

Effects develop slowly due to circuit-level adaptations

Cocaine

  • Blocks reuptake of:
    • Dopamine
    • Norepinephrine
    • Serotonin

Results in:

  • Strong increase in synaptic signaling
  • Enhanced reward and arousal
  • High addictive potential
